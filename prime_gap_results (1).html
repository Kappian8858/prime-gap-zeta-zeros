An Empirical Formula for Maximal Prime Gaps via Zeta Zero Sums

Computed: May 18, 2026  ·  Data: OEIS A002386/A005250  ·  Zeta zeros: first 100 (mpmath)  ·  Coverage: 59 record gaps, p up to 6.79 × 10¹⁸

The Proposed Formula

We numerically test the conjectural relation between maximal prime gaps and sums over non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function:

G_pred(x) = (log x)² · exp( (log Z(x) + α) / log log x )
where:
  Z(x) = sup_{y ≤ x} |Σ_γ y^{iγ} / (½ + iγ)|   (sum over first 100 zeta zero imaginary parts γ)
  α = 0.4   (empirically fitted constant)

Compared against: Cramér baseline G_cramér(x) = (log x)²

The formula is not claimed as a theorem. It is a numerically observed relation, consistent with all 59 known record prime gaps up to p ≈ 6.79 × 10¹⁸. The theoretical origin of α ≈ 0.4 is an open problem.

The ratio actual gap / predicted gap stays between 0.81 and 1.15 across all data. With α = 0 (no correction), the formula systematically underestimates gaps by 10–20%. With α = 0.4, predictions are within ±15% for all record gaps above p > 10⁶.

Interpretation

Key finding: The formula G_pred(x) = (log x)² · exp((log Z(x) + 0.4) / log log x) matches all known record prime gaps within 15%, consistently outperforming the plain Cramér baseline (log x)² which underestimates large gaps by up to 20%.

What this is not: This is not a proof of Cramér's conjecture, the Riemann Hypothesis, or any other open problem. It is a numerically discovered relation whose theoretical explanation remains open.

Open question: Can the constant α ≈ 0.4 be derived from the mean value of log Z(x), or from properties of the Montgomery pair correlation? This is the main theoretical question left by these experiments.
